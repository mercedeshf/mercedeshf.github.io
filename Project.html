Crime and Security in Mexico

An analysis of crime trends and public security challenges, 2010-2024

Mercedes Hernández Ferrer | LSE Data Visualization Project

Project Overview

This project examines Mexico's public security landscape through comprehensive data visualization, addressing the critical question: Has Mexico improved public security overall, or do falling property crimes mask the persistence of high-impact violent crime?

Crime Trends

How have different categories of crime evolved over the past decade?

International Context

Data Visualizations

01

International Homicide Rate Comparison

Comparing intentional homicide rates per 100,000 people across global regions and select countries (2010-2023). Data sourced from the World Bank API.

Key Insights

  • Mexico's homicide rate peaked around 2018-2019
  • Colombia shows a declining trend over the period
  • Latin America & Caribbean region remains above global average
02

Theft Crime Trends in Mexico

Annual reported theft incidents from 2015-2024, showing a significant decline in property crimes.

Key Insights

  • Peak theft incidents occurred in 2017-2018 (over 800,000 annually)
  • Steady decline from 2019 onwards
  • 2024 shows theft crimes at approximately 540,000 incidents
03

High-Impact Crime Trends

Tracking homicides, extortion, kidnapping, and feminicide—crimes with the most severe impact on public security and wellbeing.

Key Insights

  • Homicides remain elevated compared to 2015 baseline
  • Feminicide shows persistent high levels
  • Kidnapping has declined significantly
  • Extortion trends remain concerning
04

Long-Term Homicide Trends (1990-2024)

Historical perspective on homicide rates per 100,000 inhabitants in Mexico, showing cycles of violence over three decades.

Key Insights

  • Sharp decline in the 1990s from peak levels
  • Historic low around 2007
  • Dramatic increase starting 2008 ("drug war" period)
  • Recent years show rates similar to early 1990s

Key Findings

01

The Paradox of Progress

While property crimes like theft have declined significantly since their 2017-2018 peak, high-impact violent crimes remain elevated. This creates a misleading narrative where aggregate crime statistics may suggest improvement while the most severe public safety challenges persist.

02

Regional Context Matters

Mexico's homicide rate, while showing some recent decline, remains significantly higher than the global average and comparable to other Latin American countries with serious security challenges. The regional context reveals systemic issues that transcend individual country policies.

03

Historical Cycles

The long-term data (1990-2024) reveals cyclical patterns of violence in Mexico, with current homicide rates comparable to levels seen in the early 1990s. This historical perspective suggests that recent changes may be part of longer-term patterns rather than fundamental shifts in public security.

Conclusion

The data challenge simple narratives about Mexico's public security. While declining property crimes represent genuine progress, the persistence of high-impact violent crimes—particularly homicides and feminicides—indicates that fundamental security challenges remain unresolved. Effective policy must address these violent crimes specifically rather than relying on aggregate statistics that may obscure ongoing crises.

Methodology & Data Sources

API

World Bank Open Data API

International homicide rate comparisons sourced from the World Bank's Development Indicators database. The API provides standardized data across countries, enabling reliable cross-national analysis.

Indicator: VC.IHR.PSRC.P5 (Intentional homicides per 100,000 people)

Time Period: 2010-2023

Countries/Regions: World, Mexico, Colombia, Brazil, Chile, United Kingdom, Latin America & Caribbean

DATA

INEGI

Domestic crime statistics from Mexico's official statistical agency (Instituto Nacional de Estadística y Geografía), providing detailed breakdowns of crime types and long-term historical data.

Dataset: Incidencia Delictiva (Crime Incidence)

Time Period: 1990-2024

Crime Categories: Theft, homicide, extortion, kidnapping, feminicide
